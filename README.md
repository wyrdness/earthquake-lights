## Overview

Earthquake lights (EQL) are anomalous luminous phenomena reported in the sky or near the ground in association with seismic activity, often described as glowing orbs, sheet-like flashes, blue-white discharges, flame-like columns, or aurora-like glows. Reports go back more than a thousand years (the 869 Jōgan earthquake in Japan is among the earliest written accounts) and continued sporadically through the 19th and 20th centuries before becoming photographically documented in the 1965–1967 Matsushiro swarm in Nagano, Japan. Modern scientific work distinguishes preseismic lights (occurring seconds to weeks before a major earthquake, generally close to the epicentre) from coseismic lights (occurring during seismic-wave passage, sometimes hundreds of kilometres away). The 2014 study by Robert Thériault and Friedemann Freund in 'Seismological Research Letters' compiled 65 documented cases since 1600 and proposed that the lights occur predominantly along subvertical faults in rifting environments. The leading mechanistic hypotheses involve mobilisation of positive holes (peroxy-bond defects in stressed igneous rock; Freund), piezoelectric generation in quartz-rich rocks, and triboelectric / triboluminescent processes (Shinbrot et al., 2014). Mainstream geophysics now broadly accepts that EQL exist as a real if poorly-understood phenomenon, while remaining cautious about specific reports.

## Theories & Explanations

### Scientific

**Positive-hole (p-hole) charge carriers (Freund mechanism)** _(Friedemann Freund, Robert Thériault, John Scoville)_

Stressed igneous rocks containing peroxy bonds release positive-hole charge carriers under compression; these carriers travel through the rock to the surface and ionise air, producing plasma luminescence at the ground or in the lower atmosphere.

**Piezoelectric generation** _(Various 1960s–1980s authors)_

Quartz-rich rocks under tectonic stress generate strong electric fields by the piezoelectric effect, occasionally sufficient to drive air breakdown and luminescence.

**Triboelectric / triboluminescent discharge (Shinbrot et al. 2014)** _(Troy Shinbrot (Rutgers University))_

Rubbing of identical rock layers under shear during fault movement generates voltage spikes and luminescence even without piezoelectric or peroxy-bond mechanisms.

**Ionosphere / magnetic-field disturbance** _(Various 20th-century seismologists)_

Local disruption of Earth's magnetic field associated with seismic activity can produce ionospheric radiative recombination resembling auroral glow.

### Skeptical

**Misidentification of conventional phenomena** _(United States Geological Survey, Brian Dunning, Robert Sheaffer)_

Many alleged EQL reports are explained by lightning from concurrent storms, electrical arcing of swayed power lines, transformer flashovers, or auroral activity.
